Domestic cats (Felis catus) are among the world's most popular companion animals. They were domesticated from wildcats thousands of years ago, primarily because they helped control rodent populations around human settlements. Today, cats are found in millions of households across the globe.

Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning their diet depends primarily on animal protein. They possess sharp retractable claws, excellent night vision, highly sensitive whiskers, and remarkable hearing. Their flexible spine allows them to jump several times their own height and land safely using a righting reflex that helps orient their bodies during a fall.

Unlike many social mammals, cats are generally solitary hunters. They rely on stealth, patience, and quick bursts of speed rather than endurance. Domestic cats communicate through vocalizations such as meows, purrs, hisses, and growls, as well as body language involving ear position, tail movement, and posture.

Cats spend a significant portion of the day sleeping, often between 12 and 16 hours. Grooming occupies another large part of their daily routine. Their rough tongues contain tiny structures called papillae that help remove loose fur and dirt while distributing natural oils across the coat.

Responsible cat ownership includes regular veterinary care, proper nutrition, vaccinations, and environmental enrichment through toys, climbing structures, and interactive play. Indoor cats generally live longer than outdoor cats because they face fewer risks from traffic, predators, diseases, and harsh weather.

Cats continue to fascinate scientists studying animal cognition, learning, and domestication. Although often considered independent, research shows they can recognize their owners' voices, form social bonds with humans, and adapt their behavior to household routines.
