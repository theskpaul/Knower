Space begins beyond Earth's atmosphere and contains planets, stars, galaxies, nebulae, black holes, and vast regions of nearly empty vacuum. Distances in space are so immense that astronomers commonly measure them using astronomical units and light-years.

Our Solar System consists of the Sun, eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and countless smaller bodies. Earth is the third planet from the Sun and currently the only known world to support life.

Stars generate energy through nuclear fusion, converting hydrogen into helium while releasing enormous amounts of light and heat. Massive stars eventually explode as supernovae, dispersing heavy elements that later become part of new stars, planets, and living organisms.

Galaxies contain billions of stars bound together by gravity. The Milky Way, our home galaxy, is only one among hundreds of billions of galaxies in the observable universe.

Space exploration has advanced rapidly since the twentieth century. Satellites enable communication, weather forecasting, navigation, and Earth observation. Robotic spacecraft have visited every major planet in the Solar System, while telescopes reveal distant galaxies formed billions of years ago.

Future exploration aims to establish long-duration missions to the Moon and Mars while searching for signs of past or present extraterrestrial life. These missions require advances in propulsion, life-support systems, robotics, and sustainable space habitats.
