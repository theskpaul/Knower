Animals are multicellular organisms that belong to the biological kingdom Animalia. Unlike plants, animals cannot produce their own food through photosynthesis. Instead, they obtain energy by consuming other organisms or organic matter. Animals exist in nearly every habitat on Earth, from deep ocean trenches and tropical rainforests to deserts and polar ice caps.

Scientists classify animals based on their evolutionary relationships, body structures, and genetic characteristics. Major groups include mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, fish, and invertebrates. Mammals are distinguished by hair or fur and the production of milk to feed their young. Birds possess feathers and lay hard-shelled eggs. Reptiles typically have dry scales, while amphibians undergo life stages both in water and on land.

Animals have evolved specialized adaptations to survive in different environments. Camouflage helps prey avoid predators, while sharp claws, venom, or speed aid hunters. Some species migrate thousands of kilometers each year in search of food or suitable breeding grounds. Others hibernate or enter dormant states during harsh seasons.

Communication among animals takes many forms. Birds sing to attract mates, wolves howl to coordinate their packs, bees perform dances to indicate food locations, and dolphins use complex vocalizations and echolocation. Social animals often form groups that improve protection, hunting efficiency, or offspring care.

Humans have relied on animals throughout history for food, transportation, agriculture, companionship, and scientific research. Conservation efforts now focus on protecting endangered species whose populations have declined due to habitat destruction, pollution, climate change, and illegal hunting. Understanding animal behavior and ecology is essential for preserving biodiversity and maintaining healthy ecosystems.
