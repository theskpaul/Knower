History is the study of past human events through written records, archaeology, artifacts, and oral traditions. By examining historical evidence, researchers reconstruct how civilizations developed, interacted, and changed over time.

Early human societies relied on hunting and gathering before gradually adopting agriculture around ten thousand years ago. Farming enabled permanent settlements, population growth, specialization of labor, and the rise of cities.

Ancient civilizations such as Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, and China developed writing systems, legal codes, engineering achievements, and organized governments. Later civilizations expanded trade networks that connected distant cultures across continents.

The Middle Ages witnessed the growth of kingdoms, religious institutions, universities, and long-distance commerce. The Renaissance encouraged renewed interest in science, mathematics, literature, and art, laying foundations for modern scientific inquiry.

The Industrial Revolution transformed economies through mechanization, steam power, and factory production. Improved transportation accelerated trade while urbanization reshaped societies worldwide.

Modern history includes global conflicts, decolonization, rapid technological progress, and international cooperation. Historians continually revise interpretations as new evidence emerges, making history an evolving field rather than a fixed collection of facts.
