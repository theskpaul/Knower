Birds are warm-blooded vertebrates distinguished by feathers, beaks, wings, and egg-laying reproduction. More than ten thousand bird species inhabit environments ranging from tropical forests and wetlands to mountains, deserts, and urban areas.

Feathers provide insulation, waterproofing, camouflage, and the aerodynamic surfaces necessary for flight. Although most birds can fly, several species such as ostriches, emus, kiwis, and penguins have evolved alternative adaptations suited to their environments.

Bird skeletons are lightweight yet strong because many bones contain air spaces. Efficient lungs and specialized air sacs allow birds to extract oxygen more effectively than many other vertebrates, supporting the high energy demands of flight.

Birds display remarkable diversity in feeding strategies. Eagles hunt mammals and fish, hummingbirds consume nectar, woodpeckers search for insects inside trees, and vultures recycle nutrients by feeding on carrion. These ecological roles contribute to healthy ecosystems.

Migration is one of the most impressive behaviors in the animal kingdom. Some birds travel thousands of kilometers between breeding and wintering grounds using celestial navigation, Earth's magnetic field, landmarks, and environmental cues.

Birdsong serves many purposes, including attracting mates, defending territory, and maintaining communication within flocks. Scientists study bird vocalizations to better understand learning, memory, and animal communication.

Human activities have affected many bird populations through habitat loss, pollution, invasive species, and climate change. Conservation organizations work to protect important nesting habitats and migration routes while promoting sustainable environmental practices.
