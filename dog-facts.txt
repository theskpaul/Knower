On average, dogs sleep between 12 and 14 hours a day, though puppies and senior dogs can sleep up to 18 or 20 hours.
Unlike cats, dogs are not strict carnivores. Thousands of years of living alongside humans have left dogs with a digestive system that can break down starches and other plant matter far better than a cat's can.
A dog's sense of smell is estimated to be between 10,000 and 100,000 times more sensitive than a human's, depending on the breed and the shape of its snout.
Dogs make roughly 10 distinct vocal sounds once barks, growls, whines, and howls are counted separately, far fewer than the 100 or so sounds cats are capable of making.
A dog's nose print is unique to each individual, just like a human fingerprint, and a few organizations have experimented with using it for identification.
There are more than 75 million pet dogs in the United States, more than in any other country in the world.
Genetic studies suggest dogs were domesticated from wolves somewhere between 15,000 and 40,000 years ago, making them the first species humans are believed to have domesticated.
The oldest confirmed dog burial, found alongside human remains in Bonn-Oberkassel, Germany, dates back roughly 14,000 years.
The Basenji and the Saluki are often cited among the oldest dog breeds, with genetic lineages that may have stayed relatively unchanged for thousands of years.
A group of dogs is called a "pack," a term borrowed directly from the social structure of their wolf ancestors.
Dogs were domesticated long before cats; cats are thought to have started living alongside humans around 9,500 years ago, well after dogs had already become working partners.
Unlike most cats, the vast majority of dog breeds were shaped almost entirely through selective breeding for specific jobs: herding, guarding, hunting, retrieving, or simply companionship.
Dogs and humans share unusually similar facial muscles around the eyes, which is part of why dogs can produce an expression resembling raised "puppy dog eyebrows" that wolves cannot.
While dogs are often called "man's best friend," wolves and early dogs may have first approached human camps on their own, scavenging scraps, rather than being actively tamed by people.
There are an estimated one billion dogs in the world today, counting strays, feral populations, and pets, making them one of the most widespread land mammals on Earth.
During the Middle Ages in parts of Europe, dogs were sometimes put on trial in mock court proceedings for crimes like livestock killing, a practice historians link to broader medieval beliefs about animal moral responsibility.
In ancient Rome, the phrase "cave canem," meaning "beware of the dog," was mosaicked onto the floors of homes to warn visitors, much like a modern yard sign.
Laika, a stray picked up off the streets of Moscow, became the first animal to orbit Earth aboard Sputnik 2 in November 1957; she did not survive the flight, a fact Soviet officials did not publicly acknowledge for decades.
Two later Soviet space dogs, Belka and Strelka, orbited Earth in 1960 and returned home safely, becoming the first orbital travelers of any species, human or animal, to survive the trip.
Hachiko, an Akita in 1920s Tokyo, kept returning to a train station to wait for his deceased owner nearly every day for almost a decade, a story that inspired statues, books, and films about canine loyalty.
During both World Wars, dogs served as messengers, sentries, and mascots; Sergeant Stubby, the most decorated American war dog of World War I, was credited with detecting gas attacks and locating wounded soldiers.
The first formal guide dog training school was founded in Potsdam, Germany, in 1916 to help soldiers who had been blinded by mustard gas, laying the groundwork for the modern guide dog movement.
Balto and Togo, two Siberian Huskies, led separate legs of a 1925 relay across Alaska to deliver diphtheria antitoxin to the town of Nome, a journey now commemorated annually by the Iditarod sled dog race.
Greyfriars Bobby, a Skye Terrier in 19th-century Edinburgh, reportedly spent 14 years guarding his owner's grave, and a statue near the cemetery still honors him today.
Owney, a stray terrier mix adopted by U.S. railway mail clerks in the 1880s, rode mail trains across the country for nine years and is credited with helping boost public confidence in mail security.
In ancient Egyptian belief, the jackal-headed god Anubis watched over the dead, and dogs themselves were sometimes mummified and entombed alongside their human companions.
In Greek mythology, Cerberus, a three-headed hound, guarded the gates of the underworld, keeping the dead from leaving and the living from wandering in uninvited.
In Norse mythology, the hound Garmr was said to guard the gates of the underworld realm of Hel and was prophesied to fight the god Tyr during the events of Ragnarök.
The dog is one of the twelve animals of the Chinese zodiac, and people born in a Dog year are traditionally described as loyal, honest, and protective.
In Hindu tradition, dogs are linked to Yama, the god of death, and are honored during the Nepali festival of Tihar with garlands and treats in gratitude for their loyalty.
The American Kennel Club currently recognizes more than 200 breeds, while the larger Fédération Cynologique Internationale recognizes roughly 360 breeds worldwide.
The French Bulldog has held the top spot in American Kennel Club registrations for several years running, after the Labrador Retriever's 31-year reign at number one ended in 2022.
The Labrador Retriever, Golden Retriever, German Shepherd, and Dachshund currently round out the rest of the most popular breeds in the United States, according to recent American Kennel Club registration data.
The Chihuahua is generally considered the smallest dog breed by weight, while the Irish Wolfhound is among the tallest breeds on average.
Despite their tiny size today, Chihuahuas are believed to descend from a larger dog domesticated in Mexico long before European contact.
The Xoloitzcuintli and the Chinese Crested are naturally hairless breeds due to a shared genetic mutation, and were historically valued in part for the warmth their skin gave off when held close.
Dalmatian puppies are born completely white; their characteristic spots develop gradually over the first few weeks of life.
Dachshunds were originally bred in Germany to hunt badgers, which is reflected directly in their name: "dachs" means badger and "hund" means hound.
Rottweilers descend from Roman drover dogs once used to herd cattle across the Alps to feed marching legions.
The Norwegian Lundehund has six fully functional toes on each foot and unusually flexible joints, traits originally selected for hunting puffins in narrow cliff crevices.
Newfoundlands and Portuguese Water Dogs both have webbing between their toes, an adaptation for strong swimming that helped them work alongside fishermen.
The popular image of a St. Bernard wearing a brandy barrel around its neck during Alpine rescues is largely a 19th-century artistic invention rather than historical fact.
Dogs are not truly colorblind, but they do see a more limited range of colors than humans, perceiving mostly blues and yellows while struggling to distinguish reds and greens.
A dog's hearing extends to roughly 45,000 Hz, well beyond the human ceiling of about 20,000 Hz, which is why dogs respond to certain whistles humans can't hear at all.
Dogs can rotate and tilt their ears using up to 18 separate muscles, helping them pinpoint the exact direction a sound is coming from.
A dog's nose contains up to 300 million scent receptors, compared to roughly 6 million in humans.
Dogs inhale scent through the front of the nostrils and exhale partly out to the sides, a trick that allows them to keep sniffing continuously without losing a scent trail.
A dog's wet nose helps dissolve airborne scent particles, making it easier for the scent receptors lining the nasal cavity to detect them.
Like cats, dogs have a third eyelid called a nictitating membrane, which helps keep the eye moist and shields it from minor damage.
An adult dog typically has 42 teeth, compared to a human's 32, while puppies are born with 28 temporary teeth that fall out by about six months of age.
A dog's whiskers, technically called vibrissae, grow above the eyes, on the muzzle, and below the jaw, helping it detect nearby objects and shifts in air currents, especially useful in dim light.
Dewclaws, the vestigial "thumb" digit partway up a dog's leg, sit higher than the other toes; some breeds, like the Great Pyrenees, are deliberately bred to have functional double dewclaws on their hind legs for traction in snow.
A dog's paw pads contain sweat glands, one of the few places on its body that perspires; most body heat is instead released through panting.
Coat color and pattern in dogs are controlled by several interacting genes, which is why a single litter can sometimes contain puppies of strikingly different colors.
Merle coloring, common in breeds like Australian Shepherds, is linked to a gene that can raise the risk of vision and hearing problems if two merle-colored parents are bred together.
A healthy dog's body temperature normally runs higher than a human's, typically between 101°F and 102.5°F.
A dog's resting heart rate ranges from about 60 to 140 beats per minute depending on size, with smaller breeds trending faster than larger ones.
Brainwave studies suggest dogs do dream, and some researchers believe smaller breeds may dream more often, though in shorter bursts, than larger breeds.
Twitching paws and muffled barks during sleep are widely believed to correspond to dream activity, much like the muscle twitches seen during human REM sleep.
Dogs tend to curl into a tight ball when sleeping, an instinct inherited from wild canids that helps protect vital organs and conserve body heat.
A dog wagging its tail does not always signal happiness; the position, speed, and even the direction the tail leans can indicate anything from excitement to anxiety to potential aggression.
Researchers have found that dogs tend to wag their tails more to the right when feeling positive emotions and more to the left when feeling negative ones.
Dogs often tilt their heads to better pinpoint the source of a sound and to get a clearer view of a person's mouth and face past their own muzzle.
The "guilty look" a dog gives, ears back and eyes averted, is now believed by many researchers to be a reaction to a person's tone and body language rather than genuine awareness of wrongdoing.
A "play bow," with the front legs lowered and the rear raised, is a dog's way of signaling to other dogs or people that what follows is meant playfully rather than aggressively.
Howling likely descends directly from wolf ancestors and can serve as long-distance communication, a response to sirens, or simply contagious behavior triggered by another dog's howl nearby.
The Basenji is sometimes called the "barkless dog" because its narrow larynx makes true barking difficult; it instead produces a yodel-like sound nicknamed a "baroo."
Dogs gather social and health information from each other by sniffing scent left by the anal glands, which carry details as specific as another dog's sex, mood, and recent diet.
Sudden bursts of frantic running and spinning, often called "zoomies," are now understood by behaviorists as a release of pent-up energy or excitement, technically termed Frenetic Random Activity Periods.
Yawning appears to be contagious between dogs and their owners, which some researchers see as a possible sign of canine empathy.
A Border Collie named Chaser, trained over several years by a South Carolina researcher, learned to recognize the names of more than 1,000 individual objects, the largest tested vocabulary recorded for any non-human animal.
Border Collies are widely regarded by trainers and researchers as among the most trainable dog breeds, based on consistent performance in obedience and working trials.
Trained detection dogs can be taught to identify the scent of certain cancers, drops in blood sugar in diabetic patients, and oncoming seizures, sometimes alerting their handlers minutes before an episode occurs.
Search-and-rescue and cadaver dogs can detect human scent buried under snow or rubble, and in some cases even decomposition gases rising from underwater.
Greyhounds can reach speeds of up to 45 mph (72 km/h) over short distances, making them the fastest dog breed and one of the fastest land animals over a quarter mile.
A Bloodhound's tracking ability is so well regarded that scent trails it has followed have historically been accepted as supporting evidence in some courts.
Bluey, an Australian Cattle Dog who worked livestock for nearly 20 years in Victoria, Australia, holds the verified record for oldest dog ever, living to 29 years and 5 months before being euthanized in 1939.
Zeus, a Great Dane from Otsego, Michigan, holds the record for tallest dog ever, measuring 44 inches (1.118 m) to the shoulder in 2011; standing on his hind legs, he topped 7 feet.
Zorba, an Old English Mastiff from London, holds the record for heaviest dog ever, weighing 343 pounds (155.6 kg) when measured in 1989.
The smallest dog breeds, such as the Chihuahua, can weigh as little as 2 to 4 pounds (1 to 1.8 kg) fully grown.
Sled dog breeds like the Siberian Husky and Alaskan Malamute have a dense double coat that lets them sleep comfortably curled up in subzero temperatures.
A dog's gestation period lasts about 63 days regardless of breed size, though giant breeds tend to have slightly shorter pregnancies and toy breeds slightly longer ones.
The largest recorded litter was 24 puppies, born to a Neapolitan Mastiff named Tia in the United Kingdom in 2004; 20 of the puppies survived.
Smaller breeds typically have much smaller litters, sometimes just one or two puppies, which is part of why toy breed puppies often carry a higher price tag.
Newborn puppies are born blind and deaf, with their eyes and ear canals typically opening around two weeks of age.
Dogs reach sexual maturity anywhere from six months to a year old depending on breed size, though giant breeds may not reach full physical maturity until closer to two years old.
A dog cannot crossbreed with a cat or any other unrelated species; despite folklore claims of "dog-cat hybrids," no such cross has ever been biologically documented.
Spaying or neutering a dog can lower the risk of certain cancers and, in females, eliminates the risk of life-threatening uterine infections later in life.
A dog's lifespan is strongly tied to its size: small breeds like Chihuahuas often live 14 to 16 years, while giant breeds like Great Danes or Mastiffs often live only 7 to 10.
The old rule that one dog year equals seven human years is a myth; aging in dogs is non-linear, and larger breeds tend to age faster after reaching maturity than smaller ones do.
Chocolate, grapes and raisins, onions, garlic, and the artificial sweetener xylitol are all toxic to dogs, with xylitol able to trigger a dangerous blood sugar crash in very small amounts.
Heartworm disease, spread through mosquito bites, can be fatal if left untreated but is highly preventable with regular medication.
Like cats, dogs can host tapeworms picked up from fleas or from eating infected rodents, and segments are sometimes visible around the tail or in stool.
Periodontal disease is extremely common in adult dogs, and regular dental care remains one of the most overlooked aspects of canine health.
Distemper and parvovirus are among the most dangerous viral diseases for unvaccinated puppies, which is why core vaccinations are typically started within the first few months of life.
Rabies vaccination is legally required for dogs in most countries, since dogs have historically been a major vector for the disease, which is almost always fatal once symptoms appear.
Unlike cats, dogs rarely develop true hairballs, since most breeds groom themselves through licking far less obsessively than cats do.
A dog's jaw can't generate the same crushing bite force across all breeds; it varies enormously, with large guarding breeds like Mastiffs capable of several hundred pounds per square inch, far above that of small toy breeds.
Some dogs show a paw preference much like the right- and left-pawed tendencies documented in cats, though research suggests the split skews differently by sex.
Polydactyl dogs, born with extra toes, do occur but are far rarer than polydactyl cats; true functional extra digits are mostly documented in a handful of breeds and individual cases.
The phrase "raining cats and dogs" has disputed origins, but one popular theory traces it to 17th-century England, where storm drains would sometimes wash dead animals through flooded streets.
The first photograph of a dog is believed to date to the early 1840s, not long after photography itself was invented.
The Hush Puppies shoe brand reportedly got its name from a Southern U.S. tradition of tossing fried cornmeal "hush puppies" to barking dogs to quiet them down.

Many working dog breeds, such as Border Collies and Australian Cattle Dogs, were bred for stamina rather than raw speed, allowing them to work for hours over rough terrain.
The Saint Bernard, the Newfoundland, and the Leonberger are among the largest dog breeds commonly kept as family pets, often weighing well over 100 pounds (45 kg).
A dog's sense of taste is less developed than its sense of smell, with far fewer taste buds than a human, which is one reason scent plays a much larger role than flavor in a dog's relationship with food.

The Dogo Argentino, Cane Corso, and Anatolian Shepherd are among several large guardian breeds developed specifically to protect livestock or property rather than to hunt.
A dog's "smile," with relaxed, slightly open jaws and a loose tongue, is generally a sign of comfort, though a tense, closed-mouth bare of teeth signals something very different.

A dog's vision is better suited to detecting motion and low light than fine detail, an evolutionary holdover from hunting ancestors that needed to spot movement in dim conditions.

A dog's body language, including raised hackles along the spine, can signal arousal from fear, excitement, or aggression, and is not on its own a reliable sign of which emotion is driving it.

The relationship between coat length and shedding isn't always straightforward; some short-haired breeds, like Labrador Retrievers, shed heavily year-round due to a dense undercoat.
